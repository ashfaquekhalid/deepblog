Samplers for Flow Matching:
From Euler to Adaptive ODE Solvers

1. Introduction — The Sampling Problem

In our previous deep dive into flow matching, we learned how to train a neural network that learns a velocity field transporting noise into data. Training was beautifully simple — a single MSE loss between predicted and target velocities.

But here's the thing: training is only half the story. Once you have that velocity field, you need to actually use it to generate samples. This means solving an Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE):

Starting from a random noise sample at , you follow the velocity field forward in time to arrive at a generated sample at . The question is: how do you numerically solve this ODE?

Why This Matters

The choice of ODE solver (sampler) directly determines three critical factors: sample quality (how close you get to the true data distribution), speed (how many neural network evaluations you need), and memory usage. A good sampler can generate high-quality images in 10 steps; a bad one might need 1000.

Each evaluation of is called a Number of Function Evaluations (NFE) — and since each one requires a full forward pass through a large neural network, minimizing NFE while maintaining quality is the central tension in sampler design.

In this article, we'll build up from the simplest sampler to the most sophisticated, with interactive visualizations showing exactly how each one traces its path through the probability landscape.

3. Euler's Method — The Simplest Sampler

The Euler method (also called Forward Euler) is the most basic numerical ODE solver. The idea is brutally simple: at each step, evaluate the velocity field at the current position and move in that direction for the full step.

where is the step size. This is a first-order method — its local truncation error is per step, leading to a global error of .

3.1 Geometric Intuition

Think of Euler's method like driving a car while only looking at your compass at the start of each block. You check your direction, drive straight for one block, then check again. If the road curves, you'll overshoot or undershoot because you didn't account for how the direction was changing during the block.

Euler's Weakness

Euler's method only uses the velocity at the start of each step. If the velocity field is curved (which it almost always is in practice), the method will drift off the true trajectory. For strongly curved velocity fields, this can lead to significant errors even with many steps.

3.2 Why Euler Often Works for Flow Matching

Despite being the simplest method, Euler's method works surprisingly well for flow matching — better than it does for diffusion models. The reason is that OT-CFM produces nearly straight trajectories. When the true trajectory is almost a straight line, Euler's method (which assumes a locally straight path) makes very little error at each step.

This is one of the key advantages of flow matching over diffusion models: the trajectories are straighter, so simpler solvers work better, and you need fewer steps.

4. Midpoint Method — Looking Ahead

The midpoint method improves on Euler by taking a "peek ahead" to the middle of the step. It's a second-order Runge-Kutta method (RK2).

4.1 The Algorithm

Instead of blindly using the velocity at the start, the midpoint method:

  1. Takes a half-step using Euler to estimate where you'll be at the midpoint
  2. Evaluates the velocity field at that midpoint
  3. Uses the midpoint velocity to take the full step from the original position
Intuition

It's like driving: instead of only looking at your GPS at the start of each block, you also look ahead halfway through the block and adjust your direction. This captures first-order curvature information. The cost is 2 NFE per step instead of 1.

7. Adaptive Step-Size Methods

All the methods above use a fixed step size — the same for every step. But think about it: the velocity field isn't equally complex everywhere. In regions where it's nearly constant (like early in generation when particles are spread out), large steps work fine. In regions where it changes rapidly (like near the data distribution where particles converge), you need small steps for accuracy.

Adaptive methods automatically adjust the step size based on an estimate of the local error.

7.1 Error Estimation via Embedded Methods

The most elegant approach uses embedded Runge-Kutta pairs. You compute two approximations of different orders using (mostly) the same function evaluations, then use their difference as an error estimate:

If the error is below a tolerance , accept the step and try a larger step next time. If it's above, reject the step and retry with a smaller step.

7.2 Dormand-Prince (dopri5)

The most widely used adaptive method is Dormand-Prince (the algorithm behind MATLAB's ode45, SciPy's solve_ivp, and torchdiffeq's dopri5). It's an embedded RK4(5) method:

Adaptive Methods for Flow Matching

Adaptive solvers are particularly valuable for flow matching because the velocity field has non-uniform complexity. Near (noise), particles are spread out and the field is smooth → large steps work. Near (data), particles converge to sharp modes and the field changes rapidly → small steps needed. Adaptive methods naturally concentrate their computational budget where it matters most.

7.3 Fixed vs Adaptive: The Throughput Trade-off

Adaptive methods sound ideal, but there's a practical issue in deep learning: variable step counts break batching. Different samples in a batch might need different numbers of steps, making GPU parallelism inefficient. Solutions include:

  • Use fixed steps for production, adaptive for research/evaluation
  • Determine a good fixed schedule by running adaptive once and analyzing where it places steps
  • Use a learned time schedule optimized end-to-end

8. Stochastic Samplers — Adding Noise

Everything so far has been deterministic — the same initial noise always produces the same output. But there's a family of stochastic samplers that inject additional noise during the generation process, converting the ODE into an SDE (Stochastic Differential Equation).

8.1 Why Add Noise?

Adding controlled noise during sampling can actually improve sample quality by correcting errors accumulated from the deterministic solver. The key insight comes from the connection between the probability flow ODE and the corresponding diffusion SDE. They generate the same marginal distribution at every time step, but the SDE explores the distribution more thoroughly.

8.3 Practical Stochastic Samplers

In practice, several approaches add stochasticity:

  • Stochastic Heun: Apply Heun's method to the SDE instead of the ODE
  • Langevin corrector steps: After each ODE step, run a few Langevin MCMC corrections using the score
  • Temperature scaling: Multiply the noise by a temperature factor to control diversity vs quality
When to Use Stochastic Samplers

Stochastic samplers are most beneficial when: (1) you're using few steps and the ODE solver accumulates noticeable error, (2) the model has some training error and stochasticity helps smooth over imperfections, or (3) you want more diversity in the generated samples. For high-quality models with sufficient steps, deterministic ODE samplers often produce sharper results.

9. Few-Step Generation — Beyond ODE Solvers

The ultimate goal is to generate high-quality samples in as few steps as possible — ideally 1-4 steps. Several approaches push beyond standard ODE solvers:

9.1 Rectified Flow (ReFlow)

Rectified Flow (Liu et al., 2022) is an elegant idea: once you've trained a flow matching model, use it to generate pairs by running the ODE, then retrain on these new pairs. The second model learns straighter trajectories because its training pairs are already approximately coupled by the ODE.

Each iteration of "rectification" produces straighter paths. After 2-3 rounds, you can often use Euler with just 1-2 steps!

9.2 Consistency Distillation

Consistency models (Song et al., 2023) train a model to map any point on the ODE trajectory directly to the endpoint, bypassing the ODE entirely. The consistency property requires: for any on the same trajectory.

9.3 Progressive Distillation

Progressive distillation (Salimans & Ho, 2022) iteratively halves the number of steps. Starting from a teacher that uses steps, train a student to match the teacher's 2-step output in a single step. Then repeat, using the student as the new teacher. After rounds: steps.

At large , it learns to "shortcut" multiple ODE steps into one. This allows variable step counts at inference time without retraining.

10. Practical Guide — Which Sampler to Use?

Here's a decision framework for choosing your sampler:

10.1 Quick Recommendations

Scenario Recommended Sampler Steps Why
Real-time / Interactive Euler 5-10 Minimum NFE, works well with straight OT paths
Production (quality matters) Heun 10-25 Best quality-per-NFE for most models
Research / Evaluation dopri5 (adaptive) Auto Ground-truth quality, error-controlled
Maximum quality RK4 50-100 Near-exact ODE solution
Ultra-fast (1-4 steps) Euler + ReFlow 1-4 Requires distillation/rectification

10.2 Time Schedule Matters

Beyond the solver itself, the time schedule — where you place your discrete time steps — has a huge impact. Common choices:

  • Uniform: — simple, decent default
  • Quadratic: — more steps near (noise)
  • Logit-normal: More steps near and — matches where the velocity field changes most
  • Karras schedule: optimized for specific noise level parameterizations
  • Learned: Optimize the schedule end-to-end for your specific model
Pro Tip

For flow matching models trained with the OT path (), a uniform schedule with Heun's method using 20-30 steps is an excellent default that works well across almost all architectures and datasets.

11. Implementation in PyTorch

Let's implement all the samplers we've discussed. Each one takes a trained velocity model and returns generated samples.

11.1 Euler Sampler

samplers.py PyTorch 
@torch.no_grad()
def euler_sampler(model, x, steps=20):
    """Forward Euler method. NFE = steps."""
    dt = 1.0 / steps
    for i in range(steps):
        t = torch.full((x.shape[0],), i * dt, device=x.device)
        v = model(x, t)
        x = x + dt * v
    return x

11.2 Midpoint Sampler

samplers.py PyTorch 
@torch.no_grad()
def midpoint_sampler(model, x, steps=20):
    """Explicit midpoint method. NFE = 2 * steps."""
    dt = 1.0 / steps
    for i in range(steps):
        t = torch.full((x.shape[0],), i * dt, device=x.device)
        k1 = model(x, t)
        t_mid = torch.full_like(t, (i + 0.5) * dt)
        k2 = model(x + 0.5 * dt * k1, t_mid)
        x = x + dt * k2
    return x

11.3 Heun's Sampler

samplers.py PyTorch 
@torch.no_grad()
def heun_sampler(model, x, steps=20):
    """Heun's method (improved Euler). NFE = 2 * steps."""
    dt = 1.0 / steps
    for i in range(steps):
        t_n = torch.full((x.shape[0],), i * dt, device=x.device)
        t_next = torch.full_like(t_n, (i + 1) * dt)
        k1 = model(x, t_n)
        x_pred = x + dt * k1
        k2 = model(x_pred, t_next)
        x = x + 0.5 * dt * (k1 + k2)
    return x

11.4 RK4 Sampler

samplers.py PyTorch 
@torch.no_grad()
def rk4_sampler(model, x, steps=20):
    """Classic 4th-order Runge-Kutta. NFE = 4 * steps."""
    dt = 1.0 / steps
    for i in range(steps):
        t = i * dt
        t_tensor = torch.full((x.shape[0],), t, device=x.device)
        t_mid = torch.full_like(t_tensor, t + 0.5 * dt)
        t_end = torch.full_like(t_tensor, t + dt)

        k1 = model(x, t_tensor)
        k2 = model(x + 0.5 * dt * k1, t_mid)
        k3 = model(x + 0.5 * dt * k2, t_mid)
        k4 = model(x + dt * k3, t_end)

        x = x + (dt / 6) * (k1 + 2*k2 + 2*k3 + k4)
    return x

11.5 Unified Sampler with Schedule

samplers.py PyTorch 
def get_schedule(steps, schedule="uniform"):
    """Generate time schedule for sampling."""
    if schedule == "uniform":
        return torch.linspace(0, 1, steps + 1)
    elif schedule == "quadratic":
        return torch.linspace(0, 1, steps + 1) ** 2
    elif schedule == "cosine":
        return 0.5 * (1 - torch.cos(
            torch.linspace(0, torch.pi, steps + 1)))
    raise ValueError(f"Unknown schedule: {schedule}")

@torch.no_grad()
def sample(model, x, steps=20, method="heun", schedule="uniform"):
    """Unified sampler with configurable method and schedule."""
    ts = get_schedule(steps, schedule).to(x.device)
    samplers = {"euler": _euler, "midpoint": _midpoint,
                "heun": _heun, "rk4": _rk4}
    step_fn = samplers[method]

    for i in range(steps):
        x = step_fn(model, x, ts[i], ts[i+1] - ts[i])
    return x

12. Conclusion

The choice of sampler is not an afterthought — it's a critical design decision that can make or break your flow matching model's performance in production. Here's the big picture:

  • Euler is the workhorse — simple, fast, and surprisingly effective for straight OT paths
  • Heun's method offers the best bang for your buck in most practical scenarios
  • RK4 gives near-exact solutions when you can afford 4x the NFE
  • Adaptive methods are ideal for research but tricky for batched GPU inference
  • Stochastic samplers can help when deterministic solvers accumulate too much error
  • Distillation/ReFlow breaks through the solver barrier entirely, enabling 1-4 step generation

The trajectory from noise to data is a journey, and the sampler is your vehicle. Choose wisely — and remember that the best sampler depends on your specific velocity field, your compute budget, and your quality requirements.

Key Takeaway

Flow matching's greatest advantage is its straight trajectories. Unlike diffusion models with curved paths, OT-CFM learns nearly linear flows — meaning simpler samplers work better and you need fewer steps. This is why flow matching has become the backbone of models like Stable Diffusion 3 and modern video generators.
