From action potentials to synaptic plasticity — the fundamental computational unit of the brain, explained from first principles with interactive simulations.

1. Introduction — The Brain's Computational Unit

Right now, as you read this sentence, roughly 86 billion neurons in your brain are firing in intricate patterns. Each one is a tiny electrochemical processor — receiving thousands of inputs, integrating them, and deciding whether to send its own signal forward. Together, they produce everything you experience: sight, sound, thought, memory, consciousness.

But how does a single neuron compute? What does it mean for a biological cell to process information? And how do the principles of neural computation connect to the artificial neural networks that power modern AI?

Core Intuition

A neuron is essentially a leaky integrator with a threshold. It collects electrical inputs from other neurons, sums them up (with some leakage over time), and fires an output signal when the sum exceeds a critical threshold. This simple mechanism, replicated billions of times in parallel, gives rise to all of human cognition.

In this article, we'll build up the full picture of neural computation from the ground up — starting with the anatomy of a single neuron, through the physics of electrical signaling, to the remarkable learning rules that allow the brain to rewire itself. Along the way, you'll interact with simulations that let you trigger action potentials, combine synaptic inputs, and observe spike trains in real time.

By the end, you'll have a deep understanding of how biology computes — and why understanding it matters for both neuroscience and artificial intelligence.

2. Anatomy of a Neuron

A neuron is a specialized cell optimized for one purpose: rapid long-distance communication. While neurons come in many shapes and sizes — from the tiny granule cells of the cerebellum to the meter-long motor neurons that reach from your spinal cord to your toes — they all share the same fundamental architecture.

2.1 The Four Key Components

Dendrites — the input antennae. These are branching tree-like extensions that receive signals from other neurons. A single neuron can have thousands of dendritic branches, each studded with receptor proteins that detect chemical signals. The total dendritic surface area of a pyramidal neuron can exceed 10,000 μm².

Soma (Cell Body) — the integration center. The soma is where all dendritic inputs are combined. It contains the nucleus and the metabolic machinery that keeps the neuron alive. Critically, it's where the decision to fire is made — the axon hillock, at the junction of soma and axon, has the highest density of voltage-gated sodium channels and the lowest threshold for firing.

Axon — the output cable. A long, thin fiber that carries the electrical signal (action potential) away from the soma toward other neurons. Axons can be very short (local interneurons) or extremely long (motor neurons). Many axons are wrapped in myelin, a fatty insulating sheath that dramatically speeds up signal transmission through saltatory conduction.

Synaptic Terminals (Boutons) — the output ports. At the end of the axon, it branches into hundreds of synaptic terminals. Each terminal forms a synapse — a specialized junction where the electrical signal is converted to a chemical signal (neurotransmitter release) to communicate with the next neuron.

2.3 The Numbers

The scale of neural connectivity is staggering:

  • 86 billion neurons in the human brain
  • Each neuron receives ~7,000 synaptic inputs on average
  • Total synapses: ~600 trillion (6 × 10¹⁴)
  • If you counted one synapse per second, it would take 19 million years
  • Axon lengths total ~170,000 km — enough to wrap around Earth 4 times

3. The Resting Potential — A Neuron at Rest

Even when a neuron isn't signaling, it's doing something remarkable: maintaining an electrical voltage difference across its membrane. This resting membrane potential is the foundation upon which all neural signaling is built.

3.1 The Electrochemical Battery

The neuron's cell membrane is a thin lipid bilayer — essentially an electrical insulator. Embedded in this membrane are ion channels — protein pores that selectively allow specific ions to pass through. The key players are:

  • K⁺ (Potassium) — high inside, low outside. Wants to flow out.
  • Na⁺ (Sodium) — high outside, low inside. Wants to flow in.
  • Cl⁻ (Chloride) — high outside, low inside.
  • A⁻ (Organic anions) — large negatively charged molecules trapped inside.

At rest, the membrane is most permeable to K⁺ (through leak channels). Potassium ions flow out down their concentration gradient, leaving behind negative charges (the trapped anions). This creates a negative voltage inside the cell — typically around −70 mV.

3.4 The Sodium-Potassium Pump

Without active maintenance, the ion gradients would eventually dissipate. The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump continuously works against the gradients, pumping 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in for every ATP molecule consumed. This pump uses roughly 20–40% of the brain's total energy budget — a testament to how fundamental these gradients are to brain function.

Key Concept: Resting Potential

The resting potential (≈ −70 mV) is like a cocked spring — it stores potential energy in the form of ion gradients. When the neuron fires, it releases this stored energy in a rapid, controlled burst: the action potential. The Na⁺/K⁺ pump then re-cocks the spring.

4. The Action Potential — The Neuron's Signal

The action potential is the neuron's primary communication signal — a rapid, all-or-nothing electrical pulse that travels down the axon at speeds up to 120 m/s. Understanding the action potential is understanding the language of the brain.

4.1 Voltage-Gated Ion Channels

The action potential relies on a special class of ion channels that open and close in response to voltage changes across the membrane:

  • Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels: Closed at rest. When the membrane depolarizes to around −55 mV (the threshold), they snap open within microseconds, allowing Na⁺ to rush in. They then automatically inactivate after ~1 ms, shutting themselves off regardless of voltage.
  • Voltage-gated K⁺ channels: Also closed at rest, but they open slower than Na⁺ channels (delayed rectifiers). They allow K⁺ to flow out, which repolarizes the membrane.

4.2 The Five Phases

Resting (−70 mV) — The neuron sits at its resting potential. Leak channels maintain the status quo. Voltage-gated channels are closed.

Depolarization to Threshold (−70 → −55 mV) — Synaptic inputs push the membrane potential toward threshold. If the depolarization reaches −55 mV, voltage-gated Na⁺ channels begin opening.

Rising Phase (−55 → +40 mV) — Positive feedback loop: Na⁺ channels open → Na⁺ rushes in → membrane depolarizes further → more channels open. This explosive cascade drives the voltage to about +40 mV in under 1 ms.

Falling Phase (+40 → −80 mV) — Na⁺ channels inactivate (they can't reopen immediately). K⁺ channels, which opened with a delay, now allow K⁺ to flow out rapidly. The membrane repolarizes and briefly overshoots to about −80 mV (hyperpolarization).

Recovery (−80 → −70 mV) — K⁺ channels close. Na⁺ channels reset from inactivated to closed (ready to open again). The membrane returns to its resting potential. During this refractory period (~1–2 ms), the neuron cannot fire again — this limits the maximum firing rate to about 500–1000 Hz.

Action potentials don't come in different sizes. Once the threshold is reached, the positive feedback loop always produces the same amplitude pulse (~110 mV peak-to-peak). Information is encoded not in the size of each spike, but in the timing and rate of spikes — similar to how digital circuits use binary pulses rather than analog voltages.

6. Synaptic Transmission — How Neurons Talk

The action potential is the neuron's internal signal. But how does one neuron communicate with the next? This happens at synapses — specialized junctions where electrical signals are converted to chemical signals and back again.

6.1 The Chemical Synapse

The vast majority of synapses in the brain are chemical synapses. The process unfolds in a precise sequence:

Action potential arrives at the presynaptic terminal (axon bouton).

Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open — calcium ions flood into the terminal.

Ca²⁺ triggers vesicle fusion — synaptic vesicles (tiny membrane-bound sacs containing neurotransmitter molecules) fuse with the presynaptic membrane and release their contents into the synaptic cleft (a 20–40 nm gap).

Neurotransmitters bind receptors — on the postsynaptic membrane, receptor proteins detect the neurotransmitter molecules and open ion channels in response.

Postsynaptic potential generated — ions flow through the opened channels, changing the postsynaptic membrane potential. This creates either an EPSP (excitatory) or IPSP (inhibitory) depending on the type of channel opened.

Signal termination — neurotransmitters are rapidly cleared from the cleft by enzymatic degradation (e.g., acetylcholinesterase), reuptake into the presynaptic terminal, or diffusion away.

6.2 Major Neurotransmitter Systems

Neurotransmitter Type Action Key Roles
Glutamate Amino acid Excitatory (main) Learning, memory, ~80% of synapses
GABA Amino acid Inhibitory (main) Circuit control, ~20% of synapses
Dopamine Monoamine Modulatory Reward, motivation, motor control
Serotonin Monoamine Modulatory Mood, sleep, appetite
Acetylcholine Ester Excitatory/Modulatory Muscle control, attention, memory

6.3 Electrical Synapses (Gap Junctions)

A smaller class of synapses — gap junctions — provide direct electrical coupling between neurons. These are literally pores (connexon channels) that connect the cytoplasm of two cells, allowing ions and small molecules to pass directly. They're faster than chemical synapses (no synaptic delay), bidirectional, and are used where synchronous firing is critical — for example, in the brainstem circuits that control breathing.

Scale of the Problem

Each neurotransmitter vesicle contains roughly 5,000 molecules. When a single action potential arrives at a synapse, it triggers the release of 1–10 vesicles. Those 5,000–50,000 molecules must diffuse across the cleft, bind receptors, open channels, and be cleared — all within 1–2 milliseconds. It's extraordinarily fast chemistry.

7. Neural Integration — The Decision to Fire

A neuron in the cortex receives inputs from about 7,000 other neurons simultaneously. Some are excitatory (pushing the membrane toward threshold), others are inhibitory (pulling it away). The neuron must somehow integrate all of these competing signals to decide: fire or not fire?

7.1 EPSPs and IPSPs

When an excitatory synapse is activated, it opens channels permeable to Na⁺ (and sometimes Ca²⁺), producing a small depolarization called an Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential (EPSP). A single EPSP is typically only about 0.5–1 mV — far too small to reach the threshold of −55 mV from the resting potential of −70 mV.

Inhibitory synapses open Cl⁻ channels or K⁺ channels, producing an Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential (IPSP) — a small hyperpolarization that moves the membrane away from threshold.

7.2 Spatial and Temporal Summation

Spatial summation: EPSPs from different synapses at the same time add up. If 20 excitatory synapses fire simultaneously, each producing 1 mV, the combined effect is roughly 15–20 mV — enough to reach threshold. (It's less than a linear sum because the EPSPs interact nonlinearly with the dendritic cable properties.)

The Neuron as a Computer

At its core, a neuron computes: output = f(Σ wᵢxᵢ), where xᵢ are input spike trains, wᵢ are synaptic weights, Σ is temporal integration with leakage, and f is the threshold nonlinearity. The brain's power comes not from the sophistication of this single operation, but from the massive parallelism and the adaptable weights (learning).

9. Neural Coding — How Information Is Represented

If action potentials are all-or-nothing (same shape every time), how does the brain encode the richness of sensory experience? How does it distinguish a whisper from a shout, or red from blue? This is the question of neural coding.

9.1 Rate Coding

The simplest and most well-established code: firing rate. The stronger the stimulus, the more frequently the neuron fires. When you press harder on your skin, touch receptors fire at higher rates. When a visual neuron sees a brighter light, its rate increases.

9.2 Temporal Coding

But rate isn't the whole story. The precise timing of spikes carries additional information. Evidence includes:

  • Phase coding in hippocampus: As a rat runs through a place field, the neuron's spikes shift earlier and earlier relative to the theta oscillation — encoding the animal's position more precisely than rate alone.
  • Sound localization: Your brain detects the microsecond difference in arrival time between your two ears — this requires spike-timing precision of ~10 μs.
  • Synchrony coding: Neurons that fire together may "bind" features into a unified percept (the binding problem). Synchronous spikes have a stronger effect on downstream neurons than asynchronous ones.

9.3 Population Coding

In practice, information is almost never carried by a single neuron. Instead, populations of neurons represent stimuli collectively. For example, the direction of a visual motion stimulus is encoded by the relative firing rates of many direction-selective neurons — each tuned to a different preferred direction:

This population vector decoding is used by the brain to represent movement direction in the motor cortex and has been successfully used in brain-computer interfaces to allow paralyzed patients to control robotic arms.

The Coding Debate

Whether the brain primarily uses rate codes, temporal codes, or population codes remains one of the great ongoing debates in neuroscience. The reality is likely that all three are used, with different codes predominating in different brain regions and for different types of information.

11. Learning & Plasticity — How the Brain Rewires Itself

The most remarkable feature of the brain isn't its ability to compute — it's its ability to learn. The connections between neurons aren't fixed; they're continuously modified by experience. This property — synaptic plasticity — is the biological foundation of all learning and memory.

11.1 Hebb's Rule (1949)

Donald Hebb proposed the foundational principle: "Neurons that fire together, wire together." More precisely:

Hebbian Learning

"When an axon of cell A is near enough to excite cell B and repeatedly or persistently takes part in firing it, some growth process or metabolic change takes place in one or both cells such that A's efficiency, as one of the cells firing B, is increased." — Donald Hebb, The Organization of Behavior (1949)

11.2 Long-Term Potentiation (LTP)

The biological implementation of Hebb's rule was discovered in 1973 by Bliss and Lømo. Long-Term Potentiation (LTP) is a lasting increase in synaptic strength following high-frequency stimulation. The key molecular mechanism involves the NMDA receptor — a glutamate receptor that acts as a coincidence detector:

  • The NMDA channel is blocked by a Mg²⁺ ion at resting potential.
  • When the postsynaptic membrane is depolarized (by other inputs), the Mg²⁺ block is relieved.
  • If glutamate is also present (presynaptic activity), the channel opens and allows Ca²⁺ to enter.
  • The Ca²⁺ influx triggers intracellular cascades that increase the number and conductance of AMPA receptors.

This requires both pre- and post-synaptic activity — exactly Hebb's rule! The NMDA receptor is the molecular implementation of "fire together, wire together."

11.3 Long-Term Depression (LTD)

The counterpart to LTP, Long-Term Depression weakens synaptic connections when presynaptic activity occurs without sufficient postsynaptic depolarization. This prevents runaway excitation and allows the network to forget outdated associations.

11.5 Structural Plasticity

Beyond changing synaptic strengths, the brain can create entirely new synapses and eliminate old ones. Dendritic spines — tiny protrusions where most excitatory synapses form — are remarkably dynamic:

  • New spines can appear within minutes of learning
  • Unused spines are pruned over days to weeks
  • Stable spines can persist for years — they may represent long-term memories
  • Adolescence involves massive synaptic pruning (~40% of synapses are eliminated)

12.3 What AI Misses

Several features of biological neurons have no counterpart in standard artificial neural networks:

  • Dendritic computation: Biological dendrites aren't passive cables — they perform local nonlinear operations (dendritic spikes, plateau potentials). A single biological neuron may be computationally equivalent to a 5–8 layer deep network.
  • Neuromodulation: Global signals (dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine) modify how entire circuits process information — there's no real equivalent in standard deep learning.
  • Temporal dynamics: Biological neurons operate in continuous time with memory (adaptation, facilitation, depression). Standard ANNs process static snapshots.
  • Energy efficiency: The brain achieves its computation with ~20 watts. Training GPT-4 consumed an estimated ~50 GWh. Biology is roughly 10 million times more energy-efficient per operation.

12.4 Neuromorphic Computing

A growing field of research attempts to bridge this gap: neuromorphic engineering builds hardware that mimics biological neural circuits. Chips like Intel's Loihi and IBM's TrueNorth implement spiking neural networks in silicon, achieving orders-of-magnitude better energy efficiency than GPUs for certain tasks.

The Future

The next generation of AI may draw more heavily from neuroscience. Spiking neural networks, dendritic computation, local learning rules, and neuromodulation are all active areas of research that could lead to more efficient, more capable, and perhaps even more conscious artificial systems.

13.1 Leaky Integrate-and-Fire Neuron

lif_neuron.py 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def simulate_lif(I_ext, T=500, dt=0.1,
                   tau_m=20.0, V_rest=-70.0,
                   V_thresh=-55.0, V_reset=-75.0,
                   R_m=10.0):
    """Simulate a Leaky Integrate-and-Fire neuron."""
    steps = int(T / dt)
    V = np.zeros(steps)
    spikes = []
    V[0] = V_rest

    for t in range(1, steps):
        dV = (-(V[t-1] - V_rest) + R_m * I_ext[t]) / tau_m
        V[t] = V[t-1] + dV * dt

        if V[t] >= V_thresh:
            spikes.append(t * dt)
            V[t] = V_reset

    return V, spikes

# Constant current injection
T, dt = 500, 0.1
I = np.ones(int(T/dt)) * 2.0
V, spikes = simulate_lif(I, T, dt)

time = np.arange(0, T, dt)

13.2 Hodgkin-Huxley Model

hodgkin_huxley.py 
import numpy as np

class HodgkinHuxley:
    """Full Hodgkin-Huxley neuron model."""

    def __init__(self):
        self.C_m  = 1.0     # μF/cm²
        self.g_Na = 120.0   # mS/cm²
        self.g_K  = 36.0
        self.g_L  = 0.3
        self.E_Na = 50.0    # mV
        self.E_K  = -77.0
        self.E_L  = -54.387

    def alpha_m(self, V):
        return 0.1 * (V + 40) / (1 - np.exp(-(V + 40) / 10))

    def beta_m(self, V):
        return 4.0 * np.exp(-(V + 65) / 18)

    def alpha_h(self, V):
        return 0.07 * np.exp(-(V + 65) / 20)

    def beta_h(self, V):
        return 1.0 / (1 + np.exp(-(V + 35) / 10))

    def alpha_n(self, V):
        return 0.01 * (V + 55) / (1 - np.exp(-(V + 55) / 10))

    def beta_n(self, V):
        return 0.125 * np.exp(-(V + 65) / 80)

    def simulate(self, I_ext, T=50, dt=0.01):
        steps = int(T / dt)
        V = np.zeros(steps);  V[0] = -65.0
        m = np.zeros(steps);  m[0] = 0.05
        h = np.zeros(steps);  h[0] = 0.6
        n = np.zeros(steps);  n[0] = 0.32

        for i in range(1, steps):
            I_Na = self.g_Na * m[i-1]**3 * h[i-1] * (V[i-1] - self.E_Na)
            I_K  = self.g_K  * n[i-1]**4 * (V[i-1] - self.E_K)
            I_L  = self.g_L  * (V[i-1] - self.E_L)

            dV = (I_ext - I_Na - I_K - I_L) / self.C_m
            V[i] = V[i-1] + dV * dt

            dm = self.alpha_m(V[i-1]) * (1-m[i-1]) - self.beta_m(V[i-1]) * m[i-1]
            dh = self.alpha_h(V[i-1]) * (1-h[i-1]) - self.beta_h(V[i-1]) * h[i-1]
            dn = self.alpha_n(V[i-1]) * (1-n[i-1]) - self.beta_n(V[i-1]) * n[i-1]

            m[i] = m[i-1] + dm * dt
            h[i] = h[i-1] + dh * dt
            n[i] = n[i-1] + dn * dt

        return V, m, h, n

hh = HodgkinHuxley()
V, m, h, n = hh.simulate(I_ext=10.0, T=50)

13.3 STDP Learning Rule

stdp.py 
def stdp_update(pre_times, post_times, w,
                   A_plus=0.01, A_minus=0.012,
                   tau_plus=20.0, tau_minus=20.0,
                   w_max=1.0):
    """Apply STDP weight updates based on spike timing."""
    for t_pre in pre_times:
        for t_post in post_times:
            dt = t_post - t_pre
            if dt > 0:
                w += A_plus * np.exp(-dt / tau_plus)
            else:
                w -= A_minus * np.exp(dt / tau_minus)
    return np.clip(w, 0, w_max)

14. Conclusion — The Elegance of Neural Computation

We've journeyed through the full stack of neural computation — from the ions that create the resting potential, through the explosive cascade of the action potential, across the chemical synapse, into the integrative soma, and up to the learning rules that reshape the network with experience.

The picture that emerges is both simple and profound: a neuron is a leaky integrator with a threshold and adaptive weights. This deceptively simple operation, repeated 86 billion times in massively parallel networks with 600 trillion adaptive connections, gives rise to everything the human mind can do.

Several key themes run through this story:

  • Simplicity breeds complexity: The individual computational unit is simple. The complexity arises from the connectivity and the learning rules.
  • Energy is precious: The brain has evolved extraordinary mechanisms to compute efficiently — using 20 watts where artificial systems need kilowatts.
  • Time matters: Unlike artificial neural networks, the brain computes in continuous time. Spike timing, oscillations, and temporal dynamics are not just noise — they carry information.
  • Everything is plastic: The brain never stops rewiring. Every experience subtly modifies the synaptic weights, creating the substrate of memory and learning.
