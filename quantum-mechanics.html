Quantum Physics

Quantum Mechanics:
The Rules of Reality

Wave-particle duality, superposition, entanglement, and the measurement problem — the fundamental theory that governs everything, explained with interactive simulations.

📅 March 2026 ⏱ 45 min read 📊 Beginner → Intermediate

1. Introduction — Why Quantum Mechanics?

At the turn of the 20th century, physics was in crisis. Classical mechanics — the elegant framework of Newton, Maxwell, and Boltzmann — had explained everything from planetary orbits to steam engines with extraordinary precision. Many physicists believed that the fundamental laws of nature were essentially complete. Lord Kelvin famously remarked that physics had only "two small clouds" left to resolve.

Those two clouds turned out to be hurricanes. One led to relativity. The other led to quantum mechanics — a theory so radical, so counterintuitive, and so spectacularly successful that it has reshaped our understanding of reality itself.

Core Intuition

Quantum mechanics tells us that at the deepest level, nature is not deterministic — it is probabilistic. Particles don't have definite positions until they're measured. They can be in multiple states simultaneously. And observing a system fundamentally changes it. The universe, at its core, plays dice.

Quantum mechanics is not just an abstract theory for physicists. It is the foundation of:

  • All of chemistry — bonding, reactions, the periodic table
  • All of electronics — semiconductors, transistors, the entire digital age
  • Lasers, MRI machines, GPS, nuclear energy
  • Quantum computing — the next computational revolution
  • Potentially: consciousness itself (still debated)

In this article, we'll build quantum mechanics from the ground up — starting with the experiments that shattered classical physics, through the mathematical framework of wave functions and operators, to the deepest mysteries of measurement and entanglement. Along the way, you'll interact with simulations that let you fire particles through slits, explore the uncertainty principle, and watch quantum tunneling in action.

2. The Classical Crisis — Five Experiments That Broke Physics

Classical physics made predictions that flatly contradicted experimental observations at the atomic scale. Here are the five key experiments that forced physicists to rethink everything.

2.1 Black-Body Radiation & the Ultraviolet Catastrophe

A hot object emits electromagnetic radiation. Classical physics (the Rayleigh-Jeans law) predicted that the intensity should increase without limit at short wavelengths — that any hot object should emit infinite energy in ultraviolet light. 2.2 The Photoelectric Effect

When light shines on a metal surface, electrons are ejected. Classical wave theory predicted that brighter light (more energy) should eject electrons with more energy. But the experiments showed something bizarre:

  • Below a threshold frequency, no electrons are ejected — regardless of intensity
  • Above threshold, electron energy depends on frequency, not intensity
  • Brighter light ejects more electrons but doesn't increase their energy

2.4 The Compton Effect

In 1923, Arthur Compton scattered X-rays off electrons and found that the wavelength of the scattered X-ray was longer than the incident one — the X-ray lost energy. This only made sense if the X-ray photon behaved like a particle with momentum:

Light wasn't just a wave. It had particle-like momentum. The wave-particle duality was becoming undeniable.

2.5 The Stern-Gerlach Experiment

In 1922, silver atoms were sent through an inhomogeneous magnetic field. Classically, the atoms should spread out continuously. Instead, they split into exactly two distinct beams — revealing that angular momentum (spin) is quantized, taking only discrete values. This was the first direct observation of quantum spin.

4. Wave-Particle Duality — The Nature of Quantum Objects

Perhaps the most mind-bending aspect of quantum mechanics is that every quantum object — photons, electrons, atoms — exhibits both wave-like and particle-like behavior. Which aspect you observe depends on how you look.

4.1 de Broglie's Hypothesis

In 1924, Louis de Broglie made a breathtaking leap of logic. If light (a wave) can behave like a particle (photons), then perhaps particles (like electrons) can behave like waves. He proposed that every particle has an associated wavelength:

For a baseball (m = 0.15 kg, v = 40 m/s), m — far too small to ever detect. But for an electron moving at 1% of the speed of light, nm — comparable to atomic spacings. Electron waves should produce diffraction patterns when passing through crystal lattices.

This was confirmed experimentally in 1927 by Davisson and Germer, who observed electron diffraction from a nickel crystal. de Broglie received the Nobel Prize in 1929.

4.2 The Double-Slit Experiment

Richard Feynman called it "the only mystery" of quantum mechanics. The double-slit experiment encapsulates the full strangeness of wave-particle duality in a single setup:

Setup: A source fires particles (photons, electrons, or even molecules) toward a barrier with two narrow slits. A detector screen sits behind the barrier.

Many particles, both slits open: The particles build up an interference pattern — alternating bright and dark bands. This is characteristic of waves. The waves from each slit interfere constructively (bright) and destructively (dark).

One particle at a time: Even when particles are sent one by one, the interference pattern still builds up over time. Each individual particle goes through the apparatus and hits the screen at a single point (particle behavior), but the probability distribution of where it hits follows the wave interference pattern.

Which-path detection: If you add a detector to determine which slit the particle went through, the interference pattern vanishes. You get two simple bands — one behind each slit. The act of observing destroys the interference.

The Deep Mystery

The particle seems to go through both slits simultaneously when unobserved, creating interference with itself. But when you check which slit it went through, it behaves as if it went through only one. The quantum object is neither purely a wave nor purely a particle — it's something new that has no classical analogue. The math tells us to stop asking "what is it really?" and instead ask "what will I observe?"

8. The Uncertainty Principle — Fundamental Limits of Knowledge

In 1927, Werner Heisenberg discovered that certain pairs of physical properties cannot both be known precisely at the same time. This is not a limitation of our instruments — it is a fundamental property of nature.

8.1 Position and Momentum

This means: the more precisely you know a particle's position, the less you can know about its momentum, and vice versa. If you pinpoint the position perfectly (), the momentum becomes completely unknown ().

8.2 Why It's Not About Measurement Disturbance

A common misconception is that the uncertainty principle arises because measuring one property "disturbs" the other. While this is sometimes true, the uncertainty principle is much deeper. It arises from the wave nature of quantum objects:

  • A wave with a well-defined wavelength (definite momentum ) extends over all space — its position is completely undefined.
  • A wave localized in space (definite position) must be a superposition of many wavelengths — its momentum is undefined.

This is a mathematical fact about Fourier transforms: a function and its Fourier transform cannot both be sharply peaked. Uncertainty is built into the mathematics of waves.

Not Ignorance, But Reality

The uncertainty principle is not about our ignorance of existing properties. The particle genuinely does not have a definite position and momentum simultaneously. This is one of the deepest lessons of quantum mechanics: asking "what is the particle's exact position?" when it has definite momentum is not a meaningful question. Some properties simply don't coexist in nature.

12. Quantum Entanglement — Spooky Action at a Distance

When two quantum particles interact, they can become entangled — their quantum states become correlated in a way that has no classical explanation. Measuring one particle instantly determines the state of the other, regardless of the distance between them.

12.1 The EPR Paradox

In 1935, Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen (EPR) proposed a thought experiment to argue that quantum mechanics was incomplete. Consider two particles created in the entangled spin state:

This is called a Bell state (or singlet state). Neither particle has a definite spin on its own. But if you measure particle A and find spin-up, you instantly know particle B is spin-down — even if B is on the other side of the galaxy.

Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance" and argued that the particles must have carried pre-determined values (hidden variables) all along. Quantum mechanics just didn't know about them.

12.2 Bell's Theorem (1964)

John Bell proved mathematically that if hidden variables exist, the correlations between entangled particles must satisfy a set of inequalities — the Bell inequalities:

Quantum mechanics predicts that these inequalities are violated. Starting with Alain Aspect's experiments in 1982, and culminating in loophole-free tests in 2015 (Nobel Prize 2022 to Aspect, Clauser, and Zeilinger), experiments have conclusively confirmed: Bell's inequalities are violated. Nature is not locally realistic.

12.3 What Entanglement Is Not

Despite the "spooky" label, entanglement does not allow faster-than-light communication. The individual measurement outcomes are still random — you can't control what you get. The correlations only become apparent when the two experimenters compare their results (which requires classical communication). Entanglement violates local realism, not causality.

12.4 Entanglement as a Resource

Far from being just a philosophical curiosity, entanglement is now recognized as a physical resource that enables:

  • Quantum teleportation — transmitting a quantum state using entanglement + 2 classical bits
  • Quantum key distribution — provably secure cryptography
  • Quantum computing — entanglement is what gives quantum computers their exponential advantage for certain problems
  • Quantum sensors — entangled particles enable measurements below the classical noise limit
Entanglement in One Sentence

Entangled particles share a quantum state such that measuring one instantaneously constrains the other — not because information is traveling, but because they were never truly separate. They are described by a single wave function, no matter how far apart.

13. The Measurement Problem — Interpretations of Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics is extraordinarily successful at predicting experimental outcomes. But it leaves a deep question unanswered: what actually happens when a measurement is made? Why does the wave function "collapse"? What counts as a "measurement"? This is the measurement problem, and it has spawned multiple competing interpretations.

13.1 Copenhagen Interpretation

The standard textbook interpretation, associated with Bohr and Heisenberg. It asserts that the wave function doesn't describe reality — it describes our knowledge of reality. Upon measurement, the wave function collapses to the observed eigenstate. The collapse is not a physical process but an update of information.

Pros: Pragmatic, gives correct predictions, widely taught.
Cons: Doesn't explain what a measurement is. Requires an arbitrary quantum/classical divide.

13.2 Many-Worlds Interpretation

Proposed by Hugh Everett III in 1957. There is no collapse — the wave function always evolves unitarily via the Schrödinger equation. When a "measurement" occurs, the universe branches: every possible outcome occurs in a separate branch of reality. The observer in each branch sees only their outcome.

Pros: No collapse postulate, elegant, deterministic at the universal level.
Cons: Implies a vast (infinite?) number of unobservable parallel worlds. Probability interpretation is debated.

13.3 Decoherence

While not a full interpretation, decoherence theory explains why macroscopic superpositions are never observed. When a quantum system interacts with its environment (air molecules, photons, thermal radiation), the relative phases of different superposition components become randomized extremely quickly (on timescales of ~10⁻²⁰ seconds for macroscopic objects). This effectively destroys interference without needing a collapse postulate.

Decoherence explains the transition from quantum to classical behavior, but it doesn't solve the measurement problem completely — it doesn't explain why we observe one particular outcome.

13.4 Other Interpretations

Interpretation Collapse? Key Idea
Pilot Wave (Bohm) No Particles have definite positions guided by the wave function. Deterministic but nonlocal.
QBism Subjective Wave function represents an agent's personal beliefs. Physics is about experience, not reality.
Objective Collapse (GRW) Physical Spontaneous random collapses happen naturally — faster for larger systems. Testable.
Relational (Rovelli) Relative Quantum states are relative to the observer. No absolute state of affairs.
All Interpretations Give the Same Predictions

Every interpretation of quantum mechanics agrees on the mathematics and makes the same experimental predictions. They differ only in their ontology — what they claim about the nature of reality itself. This makes the choice between them partly a question of philosophy, not physics. Some physicists argue we should "shut up and calculate"; others insist the interpretation matters for guiding future research.

14.1 Solving the Schrödinger Equation (1D)

schrodinger_1d.py 
import numpy as np

def evolve_wavepacket(N=512, dx=0.1, dt=0.005,
                       x0=-15, k0=5, sigma=2.0,
                       V_height=3.0, V_width=1.0,
                       steps=2000):
    """Evolve a Gaussian wave packet hitting a barrier
    using the split-operator Fourier method."""
    x = np.linspace(-N*dx/2, N*dx/2, N)
    dk = 2 * np.pi / (N * dx)
    k = np.fft.fftfreq(N, dx) * 2 * np.pi

    # Gaussian wave packet
    psi = np.exp(-(x - x0)**2 / (4*sigma**2)) * np.exp(1j*k0*x)
    psi /= np.sqrt(np.sum(np.abs(psi)**2) * dx)

    # Rectangular barrier
    V = np.where(np.abs(x) < V_width/2, V_height, 0)

    # Propagators
    expV = np.exp(-1j * V * dt / 2)
    expK = np.exp(-1j * k**2 * dt / 2)

    snapshots = [np.abs(psi)**2]
    for _ in range(steps):
        psi *= expV
        psi = np.fft.ifft(expK * np.fft.fft(psi))
        psi *= expV
        if _ % 100 == 0:
            snapshots.append(np.abs(psi)**2)

    return x, V, snapshots

14.2 Double-Slit Interference Pattern

double_slit.py 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def double_slit_pattern(d=0.1e-3, a=0.02e-3,
                         wavelength=500e-9,
                         L=1.0, N=1000):
    """Calculate the double-slit interference pattern.
    d: slit separation, a: slit width,
    wavelength: de Broglie wavelength, L: screen distance."""
    k = 2 * np.pi / wavelength
    y = np.linspace(-0.01, 0.01, N)
    theta = y / L

    # Single slit envelope × double slit interference
    beta = k * a * np.sin(theta) / 2
    gamma = k * d * np.sin(theta) / 2

    single = np.sinc(beta / np.pi)**2
    double = np.cos(gamma)**2
    intensity = single * double

    plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
    plt.plot(y * 1e3, intensity, color='#00d4ff')
    plt.fill_between(y * 1e3, intensity, alpha=0.15,
                      color='#00d4ff')
    plt.xlabel('Screen Position (mm)')
    plt.ylabel('Intensity')
    plt.title('Double-Slit Interference Pattern')
    plt.show()

14.3 Quantum Tunneling Transmission Coefficient

tunneling.py 
import numpy as np

def transmission_coeff(E, V0, L, m=9.109e-31):
    """Exact transmission coefficient for a rectangular barrier.
    E: particle energy (eV), V0: barrier height (eV),
    L: barrier width (m), m: particle mass (kg)."""
    hbar = 1.055e-34
    E_J = E * 1.6e-19
    V_J = V0 * 1.6e-19

    if E >= V0:
        k2 = np.sqrt(2*m*(E_J - V_J)) / hbar
        return 1 / (1 + (V_J**2 * np.sin(k2*L)**2)
                    / (4*E_J*(E_J - V_J)))

    kappa = np.sqrt(2*m*(V_J - E_J)) / hbar
    return 1 / (1 + (V_J**2 * np.sinh(kappa*L)**2)
                / (4*E_J*(V_J - E_J)))

# Example: electron, 5 eV energy, 10 eV barrier, 0.1 nm wide
T = transmission_coeff(E=5, V0=10, L=0.1e-9)
print(f"Transmission probability: {T:.4%}")

15. Conclusion — The Strangest, Most Successful Theory in History

We've journeyed from the experiments that shattered classical physics, through the mathematical formalism of wave functions and operators, to the deepest conceptual puzzles of measurement and entanglement. The picture that emerges is extraordinary.

Quantum mechanics tells us that:

  • Reality is probabilistic: Outcomes are governed by probability amplitudes, not deterministic trajectories.
  • Observation matters: The act of measurement fundamentally changes the system. Unobserved particles don't have definite properties.
  • Everything is connected: Entangled particles share a quantum state that transcends space. The universe is fundamentally non-local (in a specific, non-signaling sense).
  • There are fundamental limits: The uncertainty principle sets absolute bounds on what can be known simultaneously.
  • The classical world is an approximation: Our everyday experience of definite objects in definite places is an emergent phenomenon arising from decoherence of underlying quantum reality.

And yet, despite its strangeness, quantum mechanics is the most precisely tested theory in the history of science. The magnetic moment of the electron has been measured and predicted to agree to 12 decimal places — the most accurate agreement between theory and experiment ever achieved in any science.
